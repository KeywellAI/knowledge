## Messaging and Secret Sauce

**The problem with generic positioning:** Phrases like "built on enterprise data" sound like any other software product. Push for sharper, more provocative differentiation.

> "Because 'built on enterprise data' — that sounds like any other software product that they all claim that…"

**What resonates:** "Chat with your data" landed well with Will because it implies ease of use and interactivity — even if it's becoming commoditized, it's a better hook than infrastructure jargon.

**Will's take on the website vs. deck:**
> "I think your website's better than your deck, as far as expressing what you do."
> "I spent about an hour on the website… I got a much clearer understanding of what you do than looking at your deck."

**The unique insight to lead with:** All point solutions use the same data sets and the same building blocks. With AI, there's no moat for them anymore. The opportunity is to eliminate silos and consolidate.

> "Like the 'get rid of the silos and point solutions' — single in on those silos. It sounds like that is a word that resonates, and probably people get it."

**Recommended messaging structure:** Tell the story of how siloed point solutions evolved over time, leading to a very inefficient and costly IT environment. Then explain how you solve that.

> "Point solutions and disparate data that's evolved over time requires complex, costly, manual workflows… what's the root cause, and what's the problem? And then you can talk about how you solve the problem."

## The Healthcare Infrastructure Gap

**How Will framed the core problem after hearing the explanation:**
- Customers have data in Databricks (or AWS, Foundry, etc.)
- There's a gap between these platforms and real, turnkey healthcare applications
- Everything is generic infrastructure — nothing is turnkey or healthcare-specific
- Making it HIPAA compliant and governed is really hard on top of that

**The highway metaphor** (Will's framing, which resonated):
> "There's probably a whole bunch of little side streets you have to go down to get there. You're trying to provide a thoroughfare… the side streets versus the highway."

## Pricing Model

### Core structure
- **Initial use case implementation:** ~$100–200k to solve a specific operational problem and install the scaffolding
- **Managed services:** ~$7–10k/month (mid-market), scaling with usage
- As customers add more use cases, cost per new use case decreases because of shared scaffolding and data

### The razor and blades model
Will told the Gillette story as a conceptual anchor:
> "It's not the razor, it's the blades… [Gillette] started selling his razor… too expensive… then it dawned on him, I'll give the razor away… but it was really the first recurring revenue model… every 30 days you need new blades."

Suggested applying a similar model: charge for the scaffolding as the base, then scale fees based on usage (Databricks DBUs as the counter/metric).

> "A Databricks customer would understand how that works."

### CFO-level clarity
> "You got to have an equation. The chief financial officer can understand what his cost is going to be five years from now."
> "The thing about SaaS, it was so easy. Users, seats… it's an easy multiplication."

**Use a high anchor, then negotiate down** (the Dell story): Come in with a logical equation for a large number (e.g., $20M based on user count), then negotiate down to a real number ($5M). Sets perceived value before discounting.

> "I set the bar with a logical equation."

### Minimums
Don't rely on pure usage revenue in early deals — set minimums:
> "You can have a small, medium, large minimum… at least 50,000, at least 100,000, at least 200,000, I don't know what the number is."

### How much to charge
> "You charge just enough that that doesn't make you smile."

## How to Talk Pricing in Sales Calls

Don't offer multiple pricing options up front — it signals uncertainty. Instead, explain the model without locking in numbers:

> "We go in with a base cost for the scaffolding, and with that comes one use case, and then as you add use cases, depending on the complexity of them… it scales up… Typical customer of your size, with a couple use cases, would be X. If you really roll this out across the enterprise, you're probably looking at the range of Y — keeping it literally that simple."

Then close with:
> "When we can get a full understanding of your needs… we'll give you a very specific detailed quote. But this is how we get that."

On locking in too early:
> "Don't get worried about locking yourself in. You have to have something."

On who sets the price:
> "You don't want to ask them what the price should be. You want to tell them what the price is, and then you can negotiate. If you go in without a 'this is how we price,' people don't really associate the appropriate value."

## Sales Process and Pipeline

### Stage discipline
Every stage needs entry criteria AND exit criteria. You can't advance a deal without meeting exit criteria — be rigorous and consistent.

### Activity vs. progress
> "Don't confuse activity with progress. Getting to a next step is activity. Progress is: 'hey, that's something we're interested in, it's going to cost us some amount of money… but someone's still going to have to go get that approved.'"

### Quick starts and pilots
Don't offer them for free — even a small amount signals value and engages procurement:
> "You're keeping yourself [back] by not charging anything. People associate value… even if it's 10k, it's not much, but the other thing it does: you're engaging with procurement."

### Qualifying to the right level
Don't spend time with people who lack authority to buy. Get to the decision maker:
> "If you're at that level and they don't have the authority to do that, then we're not going to get anywhere anyway."

### Nurturing vs. moving on
> "Is it something you're interested in? Nope, move on. 'Yeah, but not right now, can we meet in August?' Sure, incubate it… once a month, send them something… It's okay to throw people out of the funnel."

### Biggest risk
> "The worst thing that can happen… you spend six months with somebody… and you find out that they really didn't have the capability to get a deal done… time is your enemy."

## SDRs, MQLs, and SQLs

**Separate MQLs from SQLs clearly:**
- **MQL** = they'll talk to you
- **SQL** = budget, authority, need, timeline confirmed

**Align incentives:** Pay a lesser amount for MQLs and bonus SDRs for SQLs.
> "A lesser amount for MQLs and bonus them for an SQL."

Use MQLs that aren't true buyers as paths to the real decision maker, but don't count them as real pipeline.

## Credibility and Crossing the Chasm

**The chasm:** Health plans are insular. Every vendor claims AI. Without recognizable logo references, you need to find "innovators" willing to stake their reputation on something new.

Will referenced *Crossing the Chasm*:
> "When you're having something new and different, there's a chasm between what they're doing now and what you want them to do… Very few people are willing to stake their reputation on crossing that chasm, so you got to find someone who is an innovator."

**What motivates innovators:**
> "Those innovators… are an up-and-coming star… they want to attach to a success story that's going to be differentiated… or there's something in their compensation or MBOs."

**How to find them:**
> "I used to have my reps read annual reports… Google any press releases, and if a VP or director was quoted… I'd send them an email… 'really loved your quote on X; we've got something that can help'… people who think that way like to talk about their thoughts."

**On competing with the AI noise:**
> "You got to get real provocative in those situations… you got to explain why you're better than them, and if it takes, great. If not, move on."

**On market size:**
> "You don't have a problem of having not enough prospects out there. There's plenty of people that need what you're doing. You just gotta find [the right ones]."
